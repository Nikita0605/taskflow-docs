# Notifications

## Introduction

Notifications keep users informed about changes that affect their work without requiring them to continuously monitor projects, boards, or individual work items. As development progresses, assignments change, discussions evolve, workflows advance, and project priorities shift. Notifications surface these events so users can remain aware of important updates while staying focused on their current work.

Rather than acting as a communication channel on their own, notifications serve as entry points to the information that requires attention. They help users recognize when something has changed and provide direct access to the associated project, work item, or discussion.

---

## How Notifications Support Collaboration

Software development is highly collaborative, with multiple team members contributing to the same project simultaneously. Notifications help coordinate this collaboration by informing users when changes may affect their work.

A notification may indicate that a work item has been assigned, a comment has been added, a workflow status has changed, or a project activity requires attention. Instead of repeatedly checking projects for updates, users can rely on notifications to identify relevant changes as they occur.

This approach reduces unnecessary navigation while ensuring that important project events are not overlooked.

---

## Distinguish Information from Action

Not every notification requires an immediate response. Some notifications are informational and simply provide awareness of project activity, while others indicate that action is expected.

For example, assignment changes, approval requests, workflow transitions, or direct mentions often require timely attention because they may affect project delivery. Other notifications, such as completed work items or general project updates, primarily provide context and can be reviewed during normal project monitoring.

Understanding the difference between informational and actionable notifications helps users prioritize their attention without becoming distracted by routine activity.

---

## Work from the Source

Notifications summarize project events, but they are not intended to replace the feature where the work is managed.

Selecting a notification should be the beginning of the workflow rather than the end of it. Open the associated project, work item, sprint, or discussion to review the complete context before making decisions or updating project information.

Working directly from the source ensures that users have access to the latest project state, related discussions, attachments, and workflow history before taking action.

---

## Build a Consistent Review Routine

Reviewing notifications continuously throughout the day can interrupt concentration and reduce productivity. Instead, establish a consistent routine for reviewing updates at appropriate intervals, such as the beginning of the workday, after meetings, or before completing a sprint task.

By grouping notification reviews into dedicated periods, users remain informed without allowing frequent interruptions to disrupt active work.

High-priority notifications should still be addressed promptly when they directly affect ongoing work or project delivery.

---

## Balance Awareness and Focus

As projects expand, the number of notifications naturally increases. Attempting to respond to every update immediately can result in unnecessary context switching and reduced efficiency.

Focus first on notifications that relate to your assigned work, projects you actively contribute to, or activities requiring your approval or response. Informational updates should support awareness, not compete for attention.

An effective notification strategy helps users remain informed while maintaining focus on meaningful work.

---

## Notifications Are Not Project Management

Notifications are designed to highlight events, not manage projects.

Planning work, updating task status, assigning ownership, managing sprint scope, and reviewing project progress should always be performed from the appropriate TaskFlow feature. Notifications simply indicate that a change has occurred and provide a convenient path to the relevant information.

Using notifications in this way keeps communication lightweight while preserving a single source of truth for project data.

---

## Recommended Workflow

A practical notification workflow consists of four simple steps:

1. Review newly received notifications.
2. Identify updates that require immediate action.
3. Open the associated project or work item to review the complete context.
4. Complete the required action before returning to the notification list.

Following this approach helps users respond consistently without allowing notifications to interrupt ongoing work unnecessarily.

---

## Best Practices

Review notifications as part of a regular working routine rather than reacting to every update immediately.

Use notifications to identify changes that require attention, not as a substitute for project tracking.

Always review the associated project or work item before making decisions or updating project information.

Prioritize notifications that affect your assigned work, approvals, or active sprint commitments.
