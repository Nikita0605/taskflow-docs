# Sprint

A sprint is a fixed-length iteration in which a Scrum team completes a planned set of work. It provides a structured timeframe for delivering incremental product value while allowing the team to review progress and adapt to changing requirements.

Sprint work is selected from the project backlog during Sprint Planning. Once the sprint begins, the team focuses on completing the committed work before the sprint ends.

TaskFlow supports sprint planning, execution, tracking, and reporting throughout the entire sprint lifecycle.

---

# Planning a Sprint

Sprint Planning marks the beginning of every sprint.

During the planning session, the Product Owner presents the highest-priority backlog items, and the development team determines how much work can be completed based on available capacity. The selected work becomes the Sprint Backlog and represents the team's commitment for the upcoming iteration.

A sprint should have a clearly defined goal that communicates the outcome the team intends to achieve rather than simply listing completed tasks.

---

# Sprint Duration

Sprint duration should remain consistent throughout the project.

Most Scrum teams work in one, two, or four-week sprints. Choosing a fixed duration allows the team to measure velocity, improve planning accuracy, and establish a predictable delivery cadence.

Changing sprint length frequently makes it difficult to compare performance across iterations and reduces the effectiveness of sprint metrics.

---

# Sprint Backlog

The Sprint Backlog contains all work selected for the current sprint.

Each backlog item is broken down into actionable tasks that are assigned to team members. As work progresses, team members update task status, log completed work, and record any blockers that may affect delivery.

The Sprint Backlog changes only when the team agrees that adjustments are necessary. New work should generally be added only if it supports the sprint goal.

---

# Tracking Sprint Progress

TaskFlow provides multiple ways to monitor sprint progress throughout the iteration.

The Sprint Board displays the current status of every work item, while dashboards and reports provide additional visibility into sprint completion, remaining work, team workload, and delivery trends.

Updating work items regularly ensures that sprint metrics accurately represent the current state of the project.

---

# Completing a Sprint

A sprint is complete when the planned iteration reaches its end date.

Before closing the sprint, the team should verify that completed work satisfies the agreed acceptance criteria and meets the Definition of Done established for the project. Work that cannot be completed during the sprint is returned to the project backlog, where it can be reviewed and prioritized for a future sprint.

Closing a sprint creates a clear record of completed work and provides the data required for future planning and reporting.

---

# Sprint Review and Retrospective

At the end of each sprint, Scrum teams typically conduct two important activities.

The Sprint Review focuses on the completed product increment. Stakeholders review delivered functionality, provide feedback, and discuss future priorities.

The Sprint Retrospective focuses on the team's way of working. Team members identify what worked well, discuss challenges encountered during the sprint, and agree on improvements for the next iteration.

These meetings help improve both the product and the development process over time.

---

# Best Practices

Plan only the amount of work the team can realistically complete within the sprint. A smaller, achievable commitment is more valuable than an overloaded sprint that consistently carries unfinished work into the next iteration.

Avoid changing sprint scope after development has started unless the change is essential to achieving the sprint goal. Stable sprint scope improves predictability and allows the team to focus on delivery rather than continuous replanning.

Review sprint metrics after every iteration. Trends such as velocity, carry-over work, and completion rates provide valuable insight into planning accuracy and team performance.

---

# Common Issues

| Issue | Resolution |
|--------|------------|
| Sprint contains too much work | Reduce sprint scope based on available team capacity. |
| Sprint goal is unclear | Define a single measurable objective during Sprint Planning. |
| Work remains incomplete at the end of the sprint | Return unfinished items to the Product Backlog and reprioritize them for a future sprint. |
| Sprint metrics appear inaccurate | Ensure team members update work item status throughout the sprint. |
